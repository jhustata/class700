terminal#

The intro to this book provides simple instructive videos on how to publish online jupyter and bookdown books, which will be the medium you use to update us each week on your progress!

You will not be able to use a mouse for most of these tasks. And so if you are not familiar with command-prompts, this is your lucky opportunity to finally confront your fears. Its really straight-forward stuff since we will provide you with much guidance. But remember that there are lots of guides out there on youtube and even ChatGPT.

Please use the discussion forum to communicate amongst each other and with your teaching team including Jason and I, but also including Vincent and Johnathan from the 340.600 class. You may use platforms beyond these for the efficiency of your team, including Microsoft teams, Slack, etc.

My personal choice for a Terminal is VS Code. Jason’s is RStudio. One may successfully use the Macs in-built Terminal (I designed this book using it) but you’ll miss out on fancy extension-packages by 3rd parties (which you may add at a later time).

Feel free to watch the vides we’ve shared. But if you’re impatient, just follow these steps and your book will be live in less than 5 minutes. You may then edit its contents:

Setup

  1. github -> create a new public repo

  2. terminal:

   $ jupyter-book create gitname
   $ git clone https://github.com/book
   $ jupyter-book build gitname
   $ cp -r gitname/* book
   $ cd book
   $ git add ./*
   $ git commit -m "whatever update to your repo"
   $ git push
   $ ghp-import -n -p -f _build/html

  1. github -> repo -> github-pages -> view deployment

Fussing

The ultimate product from all your efforts in this class is a published abstract. But not published in the NEJM, JAMA, or Lancet kind of way, but rather self-publishing your process, as it emerges.

We are in a new age where we no longer merely wait on a fully sanitized and completed project that has undergone peer review in a traditional journal. That traditional approach is fraught with several limitations.

Instead, we now show-off each current step we are confronting in what is analogous to reality-tv.

Our aims? In a word: open-science, aka \(R^3\):

  1. Rigor

  2. Responsibility

  3. Reproducibility

With more prying eyes – not just journal editors and reviewers – looking at our process, our code, our analytic decisions, our justifications, our entire workflow, we are less prone to sloppiness, fraud, and that sort of science that everyone else fails to reproduce.
